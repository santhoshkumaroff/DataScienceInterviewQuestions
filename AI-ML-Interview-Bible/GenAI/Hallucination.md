# Hallucination

## What is it?
Hallucination is when an LLM generates information that is false, fabricated, or not grounded in the provided context. The model "makes things up" confidently. Causes: training data gaps, model overconfidence, lack of real understanding, softmax probability pushing plausible-sounding tokens. Types: intrinsic (contradicts provided context), extrinsic (contradicts known facts), factual (wrong facts), faithfulness (ignores instructions).

## Why do we use it?
We don't "use" hallucination — we fight it. Hallucinations are the #1 risk for LLM production deployment. They cause: wrong medical advice, fake legal citations (lawyers cited fake cases generated by ChatGPT), incorrect financial data, brand reputation damage. Mitigation strategies: RAG (ground in real data), prompt engineering (strict instructions), fine-tuning (domain alignment), output validation, and content filtering.

## Real-Time Example 1
**Legal Filing Disaster** — A lawyer used ChatGPT to research cases for a lawsuit. Step 1: Lawyer asked "Find precedents for my case." Step 2: ChatGPT confidently provided 6 case citations with detailed descriptions. Step 3: Lawyer used them in court filing. Step 4: Opposing counsel couldn't find any of the cited cases. Step 5: Judge found the cases were completely fabricated by ChatGPT. Step 6: Lawyer faced sanctions. All citations looked real but were hallucinated. Lesson: never trust LLMs for factual claims without verification.

## Real-Time Example 2
**Medical Chatbot** — A health company deployed an LLM chatbot. Step 1: User asked "Can I take ibuprofen with my blood pressure medication?" Step 2: LLM generated "Yes, it's completely safe." Step 3: Actually, ibuprofen can interact with BP meds. Step 4: User followed advice, experienced complications. Step 5: Company faced lawsuit. RAG could have prevented this by grounding in drug interaction database. Hallucinations in healthcare are life-threatening.

## Concept Example 1
**Detecting Hallucination with RAGAS**
```python
from ragas import evaluate
from ragas.metrics import faithfulness
from datasets import Dataset

# RAG system: answer should be grounded in context
data = Dataset.from_dict({
    "question": ["What is the capital of Australia?"],
    "answer": ["The capital of Australia is Sydney."],  # WRONG! It's Canberra
    "contexts": [["Australia's capital is Canberra, located in the ACT."]]
})

result = evaluate(data, metrics=[faithfulness])
print(f"Faithfulness: {result['faithfulness']:.3f}")
# Low faithfulness score because answer contradicts context

# Another example: answer grounded in context
data2 = Dataset.from_dict({
    "question": ["What is the capital of Australia?"],
    "answer": ["The capital of Australia is Canberra."],  # CORRECT
    "contexts": [["Australia's capital is Canberra, located in the ACT."]]
})

result2 = evaluate(data2, metrics=[faithfulness])
print(f"Faithfulness: {result2['faithfulness']:.3f}")
# High faithfulness score
```
**Input:** Question, answer, and context from RAG
**Output:** Faithfulness score measuring grounding
**Explanation:** RAGAS faithfulness checks if the answer can be inferred from the context. "Sydney" gets a low score because the context says "Canberra." This automates hallucination detection. In production, low faithfulness triggers a fallback (e.g., "I'm not sure, contact support").

## Concept Example 2
**Mitigating Hallucination with Prompting**
```python
from openai import OpenAI
client = OpenAI()

# Prompt without hallucination guardrails
bad_prompt = "Explain how to fix a broken microwave."
bad_response = client.chat.completions.create(
    model="gpt-4",
    messages=[{"role": "user", "content": bad_prompt}]
)
# Might give confident-sounding but dangerous advice

# Prompt with hallucination prevention
good_response = client.chat.completions.create(
    model="gpt-4",
    messages=[
        {"role": "system", "content": """
        You are a helpful assistant. Rules:
        1. Only answer based on provided context.
        2. If unsure, say "I don't know" — do not guess.
        3. Do not fabricate information.
        4. If asked about safety-critical topics, always recommend consulting a professional.
        5. Cite sources when possible.
        """},
        {"role": "user", "content": "Explain how to fix a broken microwave based on this manual: [provided context]"}
    ]
)
print(good_response.choices[0].message.content)
# "According to the manual, do not attempt self-repair. Contact certified technician."
```
**Input:** Prompt with and without hallucination guardrails
**Output:** Safe, grounded responses vs potentially dangerous fabricated advice
**Explanation:** System prompts with explicit rules reduce hallucinations significantly. Key rules: admit uncertainty, stick to context, decline unsafe questions. Combined with RAG (providing context), this is the most effective mitigation strategy.

## Common Mistakes
- Trusting LLMs for factual information without verification.
- Not providing context (RAG) for domain-specific questions.
- Using high temperature for factual tasks (increases hallucinations).
- Ignoring confidence scores — models are confident even when wrong.
- Not having human-in-the-loop for safety-critical decisions.
- Thinking fine-tuning alone fixes hallucinations (it helps but doesn't eliminate).

## Most Asked Questions

**Q1: What is hallucination in LLMs?**
A: When the model generates false or fabricated information confidently. Example: A lawyer asked ChatGPT for legal cases; it created 6 fake cases with real-sounding names and details, all completely fabricated.

**Q2: What causes hallucinations?**
A: 1) Training data gaps (model doesn't know, so it guesses), 2) Overconfidence (LLMs are trained to always produce plausible answers), 3) Autoregressive nature (once it starts a wrong path, it commits), 4) Lack of grounding (no access to real-time or verified data).

**Q3: What are the types of hallucination?**
A: Intrinsic (contradicts given context), Extrinsic (contradicts known facts), Factuality (wrong factual claims), Faithfulness (doesn't follow instructions). Example: Intrinsic: given document says "revenue $10M", model says "revenue $15M". Extrinsic: says "Eiffel Tower is in London."

**Q4: How does RAG reduce hallucinations?**
A: By providing factual context from trusted sources. The LLM generates answers based on the provided text, not its memory. Example: Instead of guessing a company's revenue, RAG retrieves the financial report and the LLM reads the exact number.

**Q5: How does prompt engineering reduce hallucinations?**
A: 1) "Only answer based on provided context", 2) "If unsure, say 'I don't know'", 3) "Don't make up information", 4) "Cite your sources". Example: Without instruction, model guesses. With "Say 'I don't know' if uncertain", model admits limitations.

**Q6: Can fine-tuning reduce hallucinations?**
A: Yes, by training on domain-specific factual data. But fine-tuning doesn't eliminate hallucinations — the model can still make things up. Best combined with RAG and prompting. Example: Fine-tune on medical data → fewer medical hallucinations, but still hallucinates on edge cases.

**Q7: What is grounding in LLMs?**
A: Connecting model outputs to verifiable data sources. RAG is the primary grounding technique. Example: Instead of "The population is ~125M" (hallucinated), grounded answer says "According to the 2024 census, Japan's population is 123.8M."

**Q8: How do you detect hallucinations automatically?**
A: 1) RAGAS faithfulness metric, 2) LLM-as-judge (ask another LLM to verify), 3) Fact-checking against knowledge bases, 4) Consistency checks (same question multiple times), 5) Uncertainty estimation. Example: RAGAS faithfulness < 0.8 → likely hallucination → trigger fallback.

**Q9: What is the temperature-hallucination relationship?**
A: Higher temperature increases creativity but also increases hallucinations. Low temperature (0.0-0.3) for factual tasks reduces hallucinations. Example: Temperature 0.0 for extraction, 0.7 for creative writing.

**Q10: How do you handle hallucinations in production?**
A: 1) Multi-layered approach: RAG + prompt guardrails + output validation, 2) Confidence thresholds (low confidence → human handoff), 3) A/B test outputs, 4) Monitoring and alerting (track faithfulness scores), 5) Human review for critical outputs.

**Q11: What is self-RAG?**
A: The model retrieves, generates, and then evaluates whether its own answer is supported by retrieved documents. If not, it regenerates. Example: "Claim: Revenue is $10M. Verification: The document says $9.5M. Contradiction detected. Regenerating..."

**Q12: What are the most hallucination-prone domains?**
A: 1) Current events (model is outdated), 2) Niche topics (limited training data), 3) Numeric/factual data (precise numbers), 4) Legal/medical (specific regulations), 5) Personal information (doesn't know user data). Example: Asking "What was the stock price yesterday?" guarantees hallucination without RAG.
